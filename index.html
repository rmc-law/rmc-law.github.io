Current work

Structured representations in the brain

To form more complex meanings, word meanings combine in a structured manner. Can this structure be dissociated from specific word meanings? One possibility is that the left frontotemporal language system cannot distinguish these two aspects of language. If this is true, then when two words are the same but only one is embedded within a structure, we should see no differences in brain activity. We used magnetoencephalography to measure neural signals when people read, and found that the frontotemporal system responded more strongly to structure even when words are the same.

Brain mechanisms in understanding modal expressions

We can use language to talk about things that may happen, must happen, should have happened... For example, sentences like If it wasn't raining, Tariq might have gone to the beach allow one to entertain a possible world in which Tariq had indeed gone to the beach, as it wasn't raining in that world. This project investigates the neural implementation of possibility reasoning with modal verbs may and must. Our findings suggest that specific flavours of these modals are not distinguishable at the level of brain regions. Rather, factual modal do drove the right temporoparietal cortex more than may and must. We think that the right temporoparietal cortex activity reflects belief updating when expressions are factual but not modal.
