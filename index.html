Short intro

I am a research assistant working with Liina Pylkkänen and Alec Marantz. I earned my Bachelors in Linguistics from the Faculty of Brain Sciences at University College London in September 2018.

I am interested in how humans comprehend language, and specifically, how language constructs and conveys meaning. Some questions I find fascinating: What is the nature of the meaning representations language builds? What neural mechanisms build these representations? How 'predictive' are these mechanisms?

I seek out opportunities to gain experience in laboratories to explore and hone my interests. I am especially interested in learning more about other neuroimaging techniques, computational methods in modeling (lexical) semantics, and the development of language comprehension in the mind and brain.

Past projects

collaborator: yana arkhipova • supervisor: wing yee chow • undergraduate dissertation • eeg

Comprehenders can rapidly use incoming information to update their expectations about upcoming words. In particular, Ming Xiang and Gina Kuperberg (2015) showed that concessive connectives (e.g. even so) reverse expectations previously set up by context. This project investigates the timing and mechanisms underlying the processing of concessive connectives. Specifically, we ask whether our linguistic predictions are immediately reversed upon encountering these connectives.

supervisors: jesse snedeker and tanya levari • harvard laboratory for developmental studies • eeg

The N400 response in adult is modulated by word frequency, imageability/concreteness, and word predictability in context. Children's N400 appears to have greater amplitude, greater latency, and more distributed across the scalp relative to that in adults. By letting children listen to an excerpt from a story, this project (1) uses single-trial analyses to examine whether children's N400 is sensitive to these factors and how they interact, and (2) aims to build a model of how linguistic prediction develops in children.

supervisors: wing yee chow • ucl psycholinguistics lab • reading eye-tracking

Structure building during language comprehension involves dependency formation (e.g. figuring out who herself is referring to in context). This eye-tracking study explored how contextual information may interact with Binding Principle A online. Consider this example:

Before she had lunch this afternoon, Karen claimed that Henry had educated herself in foreign policy of Pakistan.

It contains contextual information that encourages the comprehender to consider an inappropriate antecedent Karen for the reflexive herself. Whereas, the following example contains information that is pragmatically neutral for any antecedents.

After achieving a great success in her exhibition, Victoria said that Rachel admired herself for making everything run smoothly.

This study examines if there is a difference in the processing patterns between sentence types, and at which stage pragmatic information comes to play a role.

supervisors: wing yee chow • ucl psycholinguistics lab • reading eye-tracking

Meanings of garden path sentences like the pirate found the treasure was cursed are often difficult to recover. The verb found has a direct-object bias. Therefore, upon encountering the matrix VP was cursed, reanalysis is required. This effect is much reduced/eliminated when the verb has a sentential complement bias, e.g. the pirate claimed the treasure was cursed. In sentences like the pirate who found/claimed the treasure was cursed..., there are two possible analyses:

  1. the pirates [who found the treasure] was cursed...
  2. The pirates [who claimed the treasure was cursed]...

This eye-tracking study examines whether the pressure to complete an incomplete dependency is strong enough to overcome the effect of verb bias. If it is, then in this test case, verbs like claim would still take the treasure as the direct object despite its bias. Consequently, was cursed would be treated as the matrix verb.

supervisors: richard breheny and chao sun • ucl experimental pragmatics lab • corpus

Scalar adjectives such as warm thought of as representing a value on a scale such as temperature. On one end of the temperature scale there is cold, while on the other hot. The use of warm often has the implicature of warm but not hot. Similarly, adjectives like pretty are argued to have the (possible) implicature of pretty but not gorgeous. This corpus study investigates how diverse scalar adjectives are as used in everyday lives.
