Short intro

I am a research assistant exploring my interests in the cognitive neuroscience of language, working with Liina Pylkkänen and Alec Marantz. I earned my bachelors in linguistics from University College London Brain Sciences in September 2018.

I am interested in how humans comprehend language, and specifically, how language constructs and conveys meaning. Some questions I find fascinating: What is the nature of the meaning representations language builds? What neural mechanisms build these representations? How 'predictive' are these mechanisms? Recent interests include modelling meaning composition computational and exploring the interaction between perceptual systems and social+affective systems.

I am currently applying to graduate programmes in Cognitive Neuroscience. I'll be attending the 11th Annual Meeting of the Society for the Neurobiology of Language.


Current projects

supervisor: liina pylkkänen • neuroscience of language lab • magnetoencephalography

Linguistic meaning composition in a given language requires for words to be combined in a systematic fashion that conforms to the syntax of that language. Language neuroscience has enjoyed successes in studying the neural bases of language using non-invasive imaging techniques, implicating a broad network of regions spanning frontal, temporal, and parietal cortices. It remains unclear whether and which regions may be selective for computational subroutines that are syntactic in nature. This project aims to tackle the challenge of dissociating syntactic processes from common confounds such as (lexical) semantic and memory effects. We do so by embedding noun lists in sentences (with structure) and longer lists (without structure). Our preliminary results show that the presence of syntactic structure increased activity in bilateral temporal cortices and inferior precentral gyrus when lexical semantics are controlled with no local combinatorics. Our next step is to examine whether this effect is driven by the global semantic context in which lists are embedded.

collaborator: maxime tulling • supervisor: liina pylkkänen • neuroscience of language lab • magnetoencephalography

Natural language allows humans to talk about things beyond the here and now - e.g. how things ought to be, could be, or may have been. For example, sentences like If it wasn't raining, Tariq would have gone to the beach allow one to entertain a possible world in which Tariq had indeed gone to the beach, as it wasn't raining in that world. This project investigates the neural implementation of this sliver of linguistic capacity, drawing from insights gained in theoretical linguistics, philosophy, logic, and psychology. Our current findings suggest that the right hemisphere plays a role in updating beliefs during discourse comprehension.

Past projects

collaborator: yana arkhipova • supervisor: wing yee chow • ucl psycholinguistics lab • electroencephalography

Comprehenders can rapidly use incoming information to update their expectations about upcoming words online. A study found that connectives such as even so reverse expectations previously set up by context. For example:

  • Liz had an exam and failed it. Even so, she went home and celebrated.

The connective even so makes celebrated a congruent word in the event of failing an exam. This project investigated the timing and mechanisms underlying the processing of concessive connectives. Specifically, we asked whether our linguistic predictions are immediately reversed upon encountering these connectives. See our results in the abstract accepted at CUNY 2019.

supervisors: jesse snedeker and tanya levari • harvard laboratory for developmental studies • electroencephalography

Lexical access in adults has been extensively studied using event-related potentials. Adults' N400 responses correlate negatively with word frequency and word predictability in context, and have a centro-parietal distribution in EEG sensor space. These two factors interact: the N400 is not sensitive to word frequency when that word is predictable in context. Children's N400 profile appears to have greater amplitude, is more sustained and more distributed across the scalp. In this project, children from 5-10 listened to an excerpt from Matilda. This project has two goals: (i) examine whether children's N400 is sensitive to the same lexical/contextual properties and how they interact using single-trial analyses to (ii) build a model of linguistic prediction in typically developing children.

supervisor: wing yee chow • ucl psycholinguistics lab • reading eye-tracking

Meanings of garden path sentences like the pirate found the treasure was cursed are often difficult to recover. This is because the verb found has a bias to take a direct object as a complement as opposed to a sentence in this example. Therefore, upon encountering the verb phrase was cursed, reanalysis is required. This effect is much reduced/eliminated when the verb has a bias to take a sentence as complement, e.g. the pirate claimed the treasure was cursed. In sentences like the pirate who claimed the treasure was cursed..., where there is a wh-word who, there are two possible analyses:

  1. The pirates [who claimed the treasure was cursed]...
  2. The pirates [who claimed the treasure was cursed]...

This eye-tracking study examined whether the top-down predictive pressure to complete an incomplete dependency introduced by who is strong enough to overcome the effect of verb bias, which is arguably bottom-up. If it is, then in this test case, verbs like claim would still take the treasure as the direct object despite its sentential-complement bias (reading 1).

supervisors: richard breheny and chao sun • ucl experimental pragmatics lab • corpus

Scalar terms such as the adjective warm can be thought of as representing a value on a scale such as temperature - cold on one end of the scale, hot on the other. When describing something as warm, one may make the inference that that something is warm but not hot. Some studies (e.g. this) found that scalar expressions of different lexical categories (e.g. modals or adjectives) give rise to this sort of inference at variable rates. This corpus study investigated this scalar diversity effect by looking at whether the proposed inferences arise from a number of scalar terms, as evidenced on the Twitter corpus.
